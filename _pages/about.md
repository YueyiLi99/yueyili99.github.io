I am a Ph.D. candidate in the Department of Politics at Princeton University and a graduate fellow at the [Niehaus Center for Globalization and Governance](https://niehauscenter.princeton.edu/). My research sits at the intersection of international political economy, industrial policy, and geopolitics, with a particular focus on how global production networks shape state behavior in an era of intensifying great-power competition.

My dissertation, *Value Chain Interdependence and Geopolitics*, examines how the structure of manufacturing supply chains conditions the way the major powers like the United States and China design industrial policy, manage trade disputes, and respond to national-security pressures. I combine causal inference with large-scale computational text analysis of legislative records, trade-remedy hearings, and WTO proceedings to bring systematic evidence to questions historically addressed through case studies. In Fall 2025, I was a visiting doctoral student at the MIT Department of Political Science.

I bring an interdisciplinary perspective to political economy. Most recently, I conducted research at the [World Trade Organization](https://www.wto.org/)'s Economic Research and Statistics Division in Geneva, contributing to the *World Trade Report 2025: Making Trade and AI Work Together to the Benefit of All*. Earlier, I worked at the *Financial Times* and Xinhua News Agency, covering global service trade and Chinese commercial policies. These experiences shape my commitment to producing research that speaks to both scholarly and policy audiences.

My job market paper, **"Manufacturing Value Chain Interdependence and Geopolitics,"** is available upon request. You can find a full list of working papers on my [research page](/publications/), and my CV is available [here](/cv/).
